Why create another political test?

Currently, two mainstream systems exist for gauging one's ideology, the spectrum approach and the scoring approach.

The spectrum approach is familiar to most people, as it uses the concepts of progressive and conservative, in other words, how one reacts to social progress to figure out their ideology. The glaring issue with this however is that progressive and conservative are relative terms. Relative not just to the time period and society, but also to the person making the assessment.

The scoring systems on the other hand; more specifically political compasses and 8Values/9Axes/PolitiScales, try to solve this by using objective scores, but in the process loses the flexibility of the spectrums by asking questions that people might not even be able to answer or might answer for different reasons. An anarchoprimitivist is not going to care what you do about sin taxes, so how do you distinguish their response from someone who just doesn't know what a sin tax is? What if someone wants higher taxes in other areas, but is a frequent smoker and doesn't want to deal with hefty taxes on their tobacco? To hammer it in, these scores are a neat novelty, but ultimately they're useless. A position on a grid tells me nothing about my ideology, just a vague idea of what similar scorers believe (which varies greatly, go figure).

How does the test work?

This test uses what I have dubbed the tree approach, which can be divided into two components: the canopy and the branches. The canopy (pictured above) consists of a triangular table that is divided into twelve leaves spread across three segments. The assessment has you complete two activities before assigning you a leaf.

From there, you will be given a set of typically less than five questions which vary based upon what your leaf is. Because these questions branch out from under the different parts of the canopy, we refer to them as branches. Do not worry if you see terms you do not understand, they will be explained as we go along the test.
